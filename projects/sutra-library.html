01.The Problem

Sutra apps split into two camps. One is raw scanned text — readers hit an archaic character and have no way through. The other is merit-tracking software cluttered with streaks, chant counters, and ambient music that turns reading into a game.

How might a sutra app respect the text and serve only comprehension?

02.Design Decisions

Three decisions shape the product. Each one is a "why", not a feature.

  • Per-character pinyin, not per-line. Sutra rhythm lives in the single character — collapsing pinyin to the line obscures the reading. All Buddhist-specific readings are hand-verified (般若 as bō rě, not bān ruò).
  • Long-press for definitions, not tap. A tap is too easy to fire by accident on a scrolling page — every misfire breaks the reading flow. Long-press preserves immersion for readers who don't need the definition, and rewards readers who do.
  • Counting features collapsed by default. Chant counters are real religious practice, but surfacing them turns the text into a score. They are present, but folded away — the app never nudges.

03.Scope & Technical Approach

Six sutras at launch, ranging from the 260-character Heart Sutra (《心经》) to the 17,394-character Kṣitigarbha Sutra (《地藏经》). The reader ships as a PWA so it works offline on phones; a Supabase backend with Google OAuth syncs reading position, bookmarks, and settings across devices. State lives in Zustand for local interactions and Supabase for persistence.

04.Reflections

Solo shipping forces scope discipline — every feature has to earn its keep against "does this help a reader understand the text?". Content curation turned out to be 50% of the real work and 0% of the lines of code: verifying pinyin against traditional Buddhist readings, punctuating Classical Chinese, proofreading variant characters. The engineering was the easy half.

Next: user-submitted sutras with community verification, and a study mode that surfaces the per-character gloss for unfamiliar characters without breaking the reading pane.
